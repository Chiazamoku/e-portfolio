Data Collection Process

Big data refers to data that is so massive, quick, or complicated and difficult or impossible to process using ordinary methods (SAS, 2018). Unit 1 of this module introduced us to the concept of big data, its technologies and management. It highlighted the 4V’s of big data; Volume, Varity, velocity, and veracity (Lecturecast, 2023). SAS (2018) further explained that the significance of big data is not solely determined by the amount of data available. The value is determined by how you use it. You can get answers that;

1) streamline resource management

 2) increase operational efficiencies

 3) optimise product development 

4) drive new income and growth prospects, and

 5) enable smart decision making by analysing data from any source. 

When large data is combined with high-performance analytics, it is possible to complete business-related tasks. However, big data must go through several processes and stages before anyone, or an organisation can generate meaningful insights from it.

Data wrangling is the process of collecting, cleaning, and organising raw data into a useful format for analysis, and it is an important stage in the data analytics pipeline (Boopathy, 2021). It does, however, present several obstacles, security concerns, hazards, constraints, and opportunities.  We also learnt and investigated the methodologies, tools, and techniques for dealing with big data such as: data cleaning, standardization, formatting, and normalisation. 

In unit 2 we were introduced to data types (strings, character, integer, floats, Boolean etc) and formats (structured, semi-structured, quasi-structured and unstructured) data (lecturecast, 2023). An understanding of the different types and formats of data made it easy to understand data collection and storage in Unit 3. According to Stedman (2022) the process of acquiring data for use in commercial decision-making, strategic planning, research, and other objectives is known as data collection. And there are different ways to collect data, web scraping tools, parse sites and collect data not easily accessible without automation or robotic assistance and Internet of Things (IoT).

The Internet of Things (IoT) has transformed the way we collect and use data from a wide range of linked devices (Tejada, n.d.). According to IBM (2015) it is a network of physical devices, automobiles, appliances, and other physical items that are embedded with sensors, software, and network connectivity to collect and share data.

IoT allows these electronic devices to interact with one another as well as with other devices that have internet access like smartphones and gateways, resulting in a massive network of connected gadgets that can exchange data and execute a variety of functions independently (McKinsey & Company, 2022). By using IoT devices to automate and optimize processes, organisations and businesses can improve efficiency, make data-driven decisions, save cost, and enhance customer experiences. 

 IoT has numerous potential uses, and its impact is already being felt in a variety of areas, including monitoring the environment in farms, regulating traffic patterns with smart cars along with other smart automotive devices, operating machinery and operations in factories, and monitoring stock and shipments in warehouses (IBM, 2015).

Although IoT, has a lot of benefits, it also poses risks and challenges such as security and privacy risks. Many IoT devices are vulnerable to hackers and other cyber threats, putting sensitive data security and privacy at risk. IoT devices can also capture massive volumes of personal data, generating privacy and data protection concerns (TWI. n.d.).

In conclusion, data wrangling is an important phase in the data analysis process, but it has its own set of obstacles, security concerns, and constraints. To assure data quality, security, and transparency, these concerns must be addressed using a combination of processes, tools, and procedures (Kazi and Jarmul, 2016). While data manipulation can be time-consuming, it also provides potential for automation and data enrichment, ultimately increasing the value of data for analysis.
