Comparing Compliance Laws

Every organisation is required to abide by specific laws and regulations, no matter the size of the industry. According to Proofpoint (2022), regulatory compliance refers to the steps that an organisation takes as part of its operations to comply with such rules and policies. The European Union General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the Economic Community of West African States (ECOWAS) Supplementary Act (A/SA. 1/01/10) are examples of compliance laws. Below is a comparison of the two laws according to key components:

 Subject Rights to Data:

The GDPR provides comprehensive rights to data subjects, including the right to access, rectify, erase, and limit the processing of their personal data, as well as the right to data portability and the right to be forgotten (European Union, 2019), while the ECOWAS Supplementary Act recognises data subject rights as well. However, the specific rights and how they are enforced may differ between ECOWAS member states.

Data Protection Authorities:

Each EU member state has a data protection authority in charge of enforcing GDPR. While in the ECOWAS Supplementary Act, ECOWAS member countries' implementation and control may differ, and some may establish specific data protection authorities. 

DPO (Data Protection Officer):

GDPR requires certain organisations to hire a Data Protection Officer. Data protection officers (DPOs) support you in monitoring internal compliance, informing and advising on your data protection requirements, advising on Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs), and acting as a point of contact for data subjects and the Information Commissioner. While the ECOWAS Supplementary Act may include provisions for the appointment of a Data Protection Officer, the specifics will be determined by the legislation of each ECOWAS member state (ECOWAS, 2010).

Cross-Border Data Transfer:

The GDPR includes special restrictions and procedures for cross-border data transfers outside the EU. Cross-border data transfers to third countries are only permitted if they comply with Chapter V of the Regulation. Chapter V includes a limited number of methods to ensure that transfers to third countries do not undermine the degree of personal data protection required by the Regulation (Parkhimovich and Varankevich, 2019). The ECOWAS Supplementary Act may include procedures for cross-border data transfers inside the ECOWAS area, although the specifics will differ between member states.

Although ECOWAS understands the need for data privacy and protection, it lacks the institutional structure to ensure that its rules are implemented across its member countries (Agelebe, 2020). This is the most significant distinction between the supplementary act and the GDPR. Agelebe argues, that the supplementary act cannot be fully implemented across member states unless there is a reform of ECOWAS.

In summary, data privacy and protection compliance necessitate that all data professionals and individuals involved in data handling understand their roles and responsibilities, as well as that effective and appropriate security measures are in place throughout data processing.
